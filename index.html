Chapter one

A goat herder, a discovery

The story begins in the highlands of Ethiopia, sometime in the 9th century.

A goat herder named Kaldi noticed his goats dancing — strangely energetic after eating bright red berries from a nearby shrub. Curious, he tried them himself. Local monks soon followed, brewing the berries into a drink that kept them awake through long nights of prayer.

That shrub was Coffea arabica. That drink was the first cup of coffee.

Chapter two

Coffee finds the world

From Ethiopia, coffee crossed the Red Sea to Yemen, where Sufi monks brewed it for late-night devotion. From there, it spread along trade routes — Mecca, Cairo, Istanbul, Venice, Vienna, Amsterdam, and eventually the colonial plantations of the Americas.

By the 1700s, coffeehouses were the intellectual engines of Europe. They were called "penny universities" — for the price of a cup, you could sit among writers, philosophers, and merchants for hours.

Chapter three · The first wave

Coffee in every cupboard

The first wave runs roughly from the late 1800s through the mid-20th century. This was coffee as a commodity — Folgers, Maxwell House, Nescafé. Vacuum-sealed cans on grocery shelves. Instant crystals you could spoon into hot water.

The goal was scale, shelf life, and consistency. Quality and origin? Mostly an afterthought. Coffee was a daily fuel, not a craft. But it became ubiquitous — a fixture of American kitchens and offices.

Defining values: convenience, consistency, accessibility.

Chapter four · The second wave

Coffee becomes an experience

Starting in the 1970s and exploding through the 80s and 90s, the second wave was about experience. Peet's in Berkeley. Starbucks in Seattle. Suddenly coffee wasn't just fuel — it was an outing. A vocabulary. A lifestyle.

Espresso drinks went mainstream. Lattes, cappuccinos, mochas, frappuccinos. People started naming their drinks like cocktails. The café became a "third place" — not home, not work, somewhere in between.

Origin still mattered less than the ritual and the brand. But for the first time, millions of people were paying premium prices for a cup of coffee — and they wanted to know what dark roast tasted like.

Defining values: ritual, branding, dark roast, the café as destination.

Chapter five · The third wave

Coffee as craft

The third wave began in the early 2000s and is where we are now. Roasters like Stumptown, Intelligentsia, and Counter Culture started treating coffee the way wine has been treated for centuries — with attention to origin, farmer, varietal, and process.

Light roasts replaced dark. Single-origin replaced blends. Pour-overs and AeroPresses replaced auto-drip. Baristas became craftspeople. A bag of coffee started telling you the farm name, the elevation, the processing method, and the tasting notes — blueberry, jasmine, stone fruit.

Defining values: traceability, transparency, craft, terroir, lighter roasts.
